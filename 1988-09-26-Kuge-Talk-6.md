#### 0:00

The 8th line is from the top, page 66.

> It is *the forming of the fruit is up to your forming of the fruit* – this is called *ripening of its own accord*. *Ripening of its own accord* means cultivating the cause and experiencing the result. There is cause in the realm of common experience, there is result in the realm of common experience. Cultivating this cause and effect in the realm of common experience, one experiences cause and effect in the realm of common experience. *Own* is the self, the self is definitely *you* – it means the four gross elements and five clusters. Because of being able to employ the *true human with no position*, it is not “I,” it is not *who*; therefore being *not compulsory* is called *on its own*. *Accord* is permission. *Ripening of its own accord* is the time of the flower’s opening and forming fruit, it is the time of *communicating the Teaching to save deluded sentient beings*.
> 
> (From *Shōbōgenzō: Zen Essays by Dōgen*, translated by Thomas Cleary.)

Yesterday, I talked a little bit about the meaning of cause and effect in Buddhism.

Usually we believe that the purpose of Buddhist practice is to turn delusion into enlightenment, or to guide ordinary people to [be] holy beings, or to turn ordinary people into Buddha. So very naturally, we try to remove delusion in order to reach the nirvana, we try to eliminate ego in order to reach compassion or wisdom. This is very common, because if you read Buddhist scriptures, very often these statements appear, here and there. 

But I think you should be careful to understand those statements, and you should understand more deeply what the Buddha says, what the key point of these statements is. Apparently these statements are to turn delusion into enlightenment by the practice of zazen, et cetera. On the other hand, practically, it is very difficult to attain enlightenment or become Buddha by practicing zazen, or *nembutsu*, or whatever it is, whatever kind of practice you do, after eliminating or removing human desires, or ego, or all delusions. It’s *very* difficult. So I think finally people are very confused, and finally people try to understand more deeply about the meaning of the practice in Buddhism. So Dōgen Zenji [talks about] the deep understanding of Buddha’s [teaching]. 

Here Dōgen Zenji [comments] on Bodhidharma’s statement, “One flower opens, with five petals, forming a fruit which ripens of its own accord.” So I think Buddhism, particularly Dōgen Zenji, emphasizes constantly that the purpose of Buddhism is not to turn delusion into enlightenment, but to let Buddha be *active*, and to *be* Buddha. 

Yesterday I mentioned that if you use the term *Buddha*, immediately we think of Buddha as something far from us. But yesterday I mentioned Buddha is not something mysterious, a deity apart from us. In other words, if you use another word, maybe [it’s] *boundless*, or vast, compassionate, soft, blossoming energy of being. Do you understand that? *[He laughs.]* This is Buddha. Dōgen Zenji uses [the term] “the whole word.” This is a *whole word*. That’s why Dōgen Zenji [says that] the self the Buddha mentions is the whole word. 

So the purpose of Buddhism is to let the Buddha be active and be Buddha. In other words, “be Buddha” means let the Buddha be *ripened*. That is Buddhist practice. 

Do you understand that it is quite different from the purpose of Buddhism based on turning delusion *into* enlightenment? 

Because 2500 years ago already, Buddha said, “We are Buddha.” What [did he mean by] this? I think whatever you do, Buddhist practice or Christian practice or non-religious practice, basically, your boundless and broad, compassionate, soft and sharp, blossomed energy of being working is always working; that’s it. That makes you alive, every day. In terms of from this point, we say, “Everyone is entitled to be.” Or we can say, “The truth to live is to live.” We can say. So everyone is qualified to *be*; whoever you are, animate or inanimate beings. 

And then if you do practice called Buddhist practice, giving rise to the religious spirit or heart called *bodhi-mind*. That is called *bodhi-mind*, called *practice*.

So giving rise to the bodhi-mind is already a result, effect. What is the cause? The cause is broad, boundless, [blossomed] energy of being. 

And then it appears under certain circumstances called bodhi-mind; it is called bodhi-mind. So what is the bodhi-mind? What is arousing bodhi-mind? Arousing bodhi-mind is bodhi-mind arises, that’s it. It’s not *you*. If “you”
 try to arise bodhi-mind, that bodhi-mind is really *something*. So you aren’t satisfied; no matter how long or how often you try to arouse the bodhi-mind, you aren’t satisfied, because the bodhi-mind you have arisen is *something*. 
 
So what is the *real* bodhi-mind? Real bodhi-mind is nothing but boundless and blossomed energy of being, which is pretty pure. Or a kind of energy which arises as the very incipient moment. *Very* incipient moment. 

Do you understand? This is in the stage of the very incipient moment, for instance, when I walk in a hallway and someone walks toward me, I walk opposite way, and then bump into each other. Let’s think of this moment, the very incipient moment. The very incipient moment has no problem: just *bump*, that’s it. But what is the human problem? *After* bumping, then we create the second moment, and the third moment, the fourth moment, very quickly, at super-speed, and *then* we fight. If you were a samurai, you would take out your sword and fight. But at the very incipient moment of bumping each other: no problem! Because I and the other person are kind of “Casper,” you know? In the comic? *[Laughter.]* Just bumping, coming [at] each other, you know, no problem. This is very pure activity. 

And then from where do we create the problem, human problem? Fighting, or argument, or hate, like or dislike, or many things occur. From where do we create?

Well, you understand this. So all we have to do is cultivate this energy which arises in the very incipient moment. Cultivating – okay? That energy which arises at the very incipient moment is called Buddha. That Buddha becomes the cause for us. If there were this kind of buddha-nature in this world or in the individual everyday life, it wouldn’t be possible to practice, to arouse the bodhi-mind, or practice-enlightenment, and nirvana. It wouldn’t be possible. 

But whoever you are, there is always a cause called Buddha. That is very pure energy, which is constantly moving at super-speed in the very incipient moment. That is great energy because it supports your life, and trees’ lives; all sentient beings are supported by this energy. 

That’s why if you stand up in this kind of nature, you immediately are impressed very much by this nature. What did you get from this nature? When you are impressed very much by this nature, what did you get? What is that? What are you impressed by? When you see your pure energy at the very incipient moment becomes one, and energy of the trees or nature arising constantly at the very incipient moment, and then you are very impressed. Because you become “Casper,” and nature becomes “Casper.” Very naturally there is communion – spiritual communion or universal communion – between you and nature, you and Buddha. That is called *kannō dōkō*. 

That’s why when you use the toilet, you should *gassho* and chant the verse. But if you think of this practice with your frontal lobe of human knowledge, you don’t believe it, because human knowledge cannot see the panoramic picture of your life working interpenetratedly or interconnectedly with all sentient beings. That human knowledge cannot see it. That’s why we don’t believe it. But if you do this kind of practice, simultaneously you become “Casper,” so-called Buddha. And then you can get into nature’s life, and nature can come into your life. And then there is communication. 

That is called *kannō dōkō*. *Kan* means “receive,” or “feel.” *Nō* of *kannō* means “respond.” *Dō* of *dōkō* is “the path,” “the Way.” *Kō* of *dōkō* is “crossing,” “come across,” “cross each other.” 

That is kind of *inspiration*. Inspiration? Inspiration goes in and out between you and nature, you and the universe. Not necessarily a particular object. If you see this big bell, there is a particular object, you are looking at this bell. But the *inspiration* goes in and out means through this particular bell, your energy is extending into the whole world. Because you can see much, much more quality of the bell, and also many, many beings in the realm of the bell. So very naturally you can see. This is *the life of the bell*, we say; *Buddha’s life*, we say. 

So, very naturally, when you stand up in this nature, all you can do to *cultivate* this cause. [...] “Cultivate the cause” means you become very calm and pure. Throwing away your thoughts and thinking, et cetera. Anyway, even for a moment, forget it, just stand up there, sit down there. And then, both nature and you open naturally and communicate with each other. 

So that’s why Dōgen Zenji says, “the forming of the fruit is up to your forming of the fruit.” So, *forming of the fruit* means, *fruit* means bodhi-mind; bodhi-mind is *fruit*. What is the cause? Buddha-nature; Buddha: original, broad, vast, blossomed energy of life, so-called Buddha. Buddha is your cause, and then the bodhi-mind, or practice and enlightenment, nirvana, are called the *result*.

So this kind of energy arises under certain circumstances; it is called sometimes *bodhi-mind*, sometimes *practice*, sometimes *enlightenment*, sometimes *nirvana*. So all we have to do is cultivate this buddha-nature, Buddha, great energy. Anyway, cultivate. We have to cultivate because if you don’t cultivate, energy comes up, but it’s *twisted*, all of a sudden. Just like I mentioned, they’re bumping each other. In the very incipient moment, no problem: you and I working in the stage of very incipient pure energies, that’s it. But next moment, the second moment, immediately something happens. So, in the second moment, in the third moment, in the fourth moment, all we can do is, try to maintain, try to cultivate, this very incipient moment, without forgetting. Try to be *mindful* of it; this is called *mindfulness practice*. 

In other words, return to silence. Constantly we have to return to silence. That silence is completely pure. But that purity has a lot of words and great energy to support, to grow all sentient beings. So that’s why it is very great, very great. This is your original energy, original nature of your life. 

So, if we say, “forming a fruit which ripens of its own accord,” it seems to be a *naturalism*. Naturalism means... what would you say... naturalism is kind of “windfall.” Do you understand “windfall”? You expect a piece of good luck, by chance. That’s *naturalism*, I say. Do you understand? Because the result ripens of its own accord. Because we have already a great energy called Buddha – so why do we have to practice? So lets depend on the great energy. *[He laughs.]* We always say “we are Buddha” – if we are Buddha, it’s not necessary to practice; let’s take a nap. *[Laughter.]* That’s already you are completely far from the very first incipient moment, immediately. And then you enjoy the second moment, third moment... more than second or third: hundreds of thousands of moments, already. Far from you, far from the origin: silence. Okay? 

So that’s why it is not naturalism, it is not the human attitude where we expect something like a piece of good luck, without doing anything. It is not. That’s why Dōgen Zenji explains this one. 

#### 26:08

Next,

> *Ripening of its own accord* means cultivating the cause and experiencing the result. 

That’s why cultivating the cause. You understand “cultivating”? What do you have to cultivate? If you say “practice” – what is the practice? What point you should focus on if you want to practice? 

This is a point. So, cultivating the cause and experiencing the result means the great energy arises under certain circumstances, it is called bodhi-mind. That bodhi-mind is the point which we should experience. Because this bodhi-mind is nothing but the Buddha. 

So, you can experience bodhi-mind called Buddha. Because bodhi-mind is very pure. We have to see always cause and effect in terms of the Buddha stage, so-called very incipient moment. You have to return to the very incipient moment always. If you use [the term] “the Buddha,” we don’t understand what the Buddha is. Buddha is far from us! But Buddha is the pure state of human existence, called the very incipient moment. Okay? Very incipient time. 

Then how do you come back to this? With full concentration. Making your mind calm, and be there firmly. And then, naturally, it is called *returning to the very incipient moment*. But the moment when you return to it, you don’t understand it, because your body and mind is already silent. So, who knows? No one. It is not the business of your perceptions. So who knows? Silence knows the silence you have been in.

Who makes the very incipient moment? No one. So, Buddha knows Buddha. Very incipient moment creates very incipient moment. Because the very incipient moment has a great energy to create. We don’t know why, how. Well, in terms of modern physics, you can explain, probably. I don’t know modern physics, but in the 21st century I think modern physics and molecular biology and the brain and physiologies, and, well, researching very deeply about human life, about what life is, what the brain is, and what the shortest time is. Anyway, they are going deeply; deep and deep and deep. So, very naturally, probably you can know it.

But to *know* the very shortest moment is different from to cultivate your energy right in the middle of that shortest moment. *To know* is a different form *to cultivate*. Okay? *To understand*, *to know* is already separated; you separate. But *cultivate* is, you must be there, otherwise you cannot cultivate. If you want to cultivate a field, you have to be there. And you should offer your whole energy to it. And then, you can cultivate. 

So no matter how long you can know that very incipient moment by virtue of the modern physics and molecular biology or brain physiology, whatever it is – everyone knows, but no one knows how to practice it. No one knows how to cultivate it. Because it is *so* pure. It is something in motion at super-speed and in the pure state. That’s why we don’t know. 

So, anyway, we have to come back to this silence. So very naturally, we can cultivate the cause and experience the result.

So, result means if you have bodhi-mind, please experience bodhi-mind as pure as you can. So, what do you mean? If you want to do gassho, do gassho with *wholeheartedness*. This is taking care of bodhi-mind arising at the very incipient moment. You can face it, and you can return to it, return to the silence of bodhi-mind. 

If you use [the term] bodhi-mind, it’s already a word, so you have to come back to the silence of the bodhi-mind, silence of the practice, silence of the cultivating. Do you understand this one? How pure it is, and also how simple it is. Okay? Everyone can do it. Otherwise, there is no way, no path to educate or to grow your life as a sportsman, or as a housewife, as a photographer, as a dancer, as a psychologist, and as a physicist. No way. We have this. Everyone can cultivate this. That’s why we can grow. 

That’s why if you do it, very naturally result is formed of its own accord. 

But you don’t know when or how your personality is mature. All you can do is just walk constantly, quietly, in the mist, so-called mist. The mist of the so-called very incipient moment. Anywhere walk, constantly. And then, you can get your [clothes] wet. Before you are conscious of it. That is what Dōgen Zenji says in the *Record of Things Heard*. 

Let’s go [to the] next. So, “*Ripening of its own accord* means cultivating the cause and experiencing the result.” So, what is the cause? What is the result? Next he says,

> There is cause in the realm of common experience, there is result in the realm of common experience. 

I don’t know if this translation is right or wrong. Anyway, *common experience* – what do you think about the “realm of common experience”? In Japanese we call it *kugai* (公界). *Ku* means “public.” *Gai* means “the world,” or “realm.” So literally, “public realm”; it means universal realm, universal world. 

So the cause we are speaking of is not the cause in terms of dualistic view; this cause we are speaking of should be seen in terms of the universal realm, called Buddha. Which means the boundless, compassionate, and sharp, blossomed energy of being. We have to see this one. 

That is a cause, and that is a result. In Buddhism we call it *inen kaman*. *In* (因) means cause. *En* (円) of In-En is “round”; round means perfect. *Ka* (果) is result, effect. *Man* (滿) of *kaman* is fullness. Fullness means perfection or completion. Just like a full moon. So, *in* is perfect, *man*. Effect is also perfect. But we usually don’t believe this; *in* is not perfect, *in* is small. Effect must be big. *[He laughs.]* That’s what we believe. 

Why do you practice? You say, “I am not Buddha; that’s why I want to have a Buddha.” Already that means you are already not the Buddha, which means a small cause. Very small. You want to have enlightenment or Buddha means a big result. That’s impossible to do then. Even in terms of the world of economics, you cannot do it; you cannot run a business in that way. Do you understand? If you want to have a big result, you have to use a big cause. Otherwise, how can you run a business? So-called success? 

The same applies to spiritual life. We should do it in that way. But usually, we don’t believe it. That’s why we have all these confusions and difficulties always coming up. Finally we say, “Buddhism does not fit me.” That is ridiculous! *[He laughs.]* Buddhism doesn’t choose you. You have to choose it. You have to choose it. “You have to choose” means how much, how deeply you enter into it. That’s it. 

In Mahayana Buddhism, we always give the same teaching to everyone, priest or lay Buddhist. That’s why we complain sometimes we need a particular teaching for priests, for lay people, et cetera. That is already we expect that Buddhism chooses *us*. So first make the principle or rules, and then, “Please, pick me up.” If we get into it, then we feel good. If there is nothing, and you try to get into the original nature of human existence, then we are very uneasy. Do you understand that one? Look at your life. We are always doing that way. 

So Buddhism never chooses you. Buddhism is completely free, from everyone. So Mahayana Buddhism is completely free from everyone. But Mahayana appears in a different way according to the different person, because how deeply you enter into it, then Mahayana appears in a different way. Do you understand?

This is a very important point. That’s why Dōgen Zen says constantly we have [to do] like this. Otherwise we don’t realize; we are obviously expecting Buddhism to choose us. 

#### 41:36

> Ripening of its own accord means cultivating the cause and experiencing the result. There is a cause in the realm of common experience. There is a result in the realm of common experience. 

So in *kaman*, which means cause is perfect, effect is perfect, both are perfect already. So there is nothing to throw away in your life, or nothing to get in your life. All you have to do is just cultivate your original energy; the original energy of a being called Buddha. And then the Buddha appears constantly in the different circumstances – called bodhi-mind, practice, enlightenment, nirvana; morning, afternoon, evening. He appears constantly. 

Then, next,

> Cultivating this cause and effect in the realm of common experience, one experiences cause and effect in the realm of common experience. 

So cultivating the cause and effect in the realm of common experience. *Cultivating* the cause and effect is not in the realm of human desire, or in other words in the realm of the dualistic sense. Because if you see the result called bodhi-mind *right now*, all you have to do is take it and return to silence of bodhi-mind. That is a [cultivated] result. Is that okay? Constantly. 

If you see this table, take it. But your activity must be silent and calm; that means you have to return to the very incipient moment of this table; that means very pure. 

Dōgen Zenji says, take a vegetable and erect a Buddhist temple. *Buddhist temple* means holy world, holy kingdom, anyway. If you buy a piece of the vegetable, you can build it up. 

How can you build it up? This is a key point you have to learn from now on, toward the 21st century. This is very important. This is a point people don’t know, they always miss it. People are always interested in something *more* than that; because it is *too* pure, it is too simple. It is seemingly not worthy of serious consideration, because daily routine, *gassho*, washing your face, greeting good morning – that is routine. We always say it’s not important, and then practice is something *special*. The religious holiness, if you become holy, what can you do? Please show us. And then you can show walking on the lake. And then people say, “Oh, you are a great holy man!” I don’t think this is [holiness]. Oh, of course, if you can walk [on water], it’s fine. But it is not universal. You have to see the cause and the effect in the realm of the universal world, where all sentient beings exist. Because all sentient beings are qualified to *be*. If you expect something *special* in order to be “holy,” you already ignore the rest of beings who cannot do it! So very naturally, there is discrimination coming up. And then if you become holy, you see the people who are not holy and are always criticizing, consciously or unconsciously, it’s coming up, naturally. 

*[Tape change.]*

... touching the strength of your Life. This life is not small ‘l’, it’s capital ‘L’. You can touch it. 

So this cause and effect which you can experience is in the realm of the universal world. 

#### 47:09

So next, 

> *Own* is the self, the self is definitely *you* – it means the four gross elements and five clusters. 

“*Own* is the self”: “Of its own” means you, the self. So if we say “self,” immediately self is limited, seen from human knowledge. But in terms of the realm of common experience, self is no longer self, because self is a kind of energy, original energy arriving constantly at the very incipient moment. So from this point, self is what? Self is no longer self. 

So next he says, “the self is definitely *you*.” So very naturally, self *is* you. Self is others. 

So it means, next he says, “it means the four gross elements and five clusters.” That is what Buddha’s teaching says, because in terms of Buddha’s teaching the self is consisting of four gross elements and five clusters, very naturally. This is a teaching, but a teaching must be alive: a teaching must be digested by your everyday life and must be alive. That is to practice continually to return to the silence of everyday routine: washing your face, having breakfast, going to work, going to school, studying, and greeting people, cleaning the rooms, anyway, constantly return to the silence of those everyday routines. Then, you can really touch the right [chord] of the self, which is exactly the same as others: trees, birds.  

Next, that’s why he says here, 

> Because of being able to employ the *true human with no position*, ...

This is a Rinzai Zen master’s statement. This is note 2, page 74. The note says,

> 2. “True human with no position” is a famous Zen expression referring to
the ego-free human. See *Being Time*, note 8.

Anyway, this is Rinzai Zen master says that the truth is coming and going from each pore of your fresh body. You should know it, you should see it in your everyday life. And then the monk says, “Where is it?” Then Rinzai immediately picks him up and shakes him, and pushes him away immediately, saying, “How stupid you are!” That is the story. If you read *The Record of Rinzai*, you will find this Rinzai statement. 

So the truth means the true human with no position, no particular level or frame which occurs after the very incipient moment. In the second moment, even the incipient moment is already a *frame*, okay? A frame, or a certain name. But anyway, after this one, then at the second moment, third moment, we create lots of frames, names, and their positions, social status – lots of stuff, words. And then we don’t see exactly who we *are*. That’s why Rinzai points out you have already true nature, very intrinsical nature of human existence, within you. So that is “the true human with no position.” 

That’s why “because of being able to employ the *true human with no position*”: we have to *employ* this true human with no position. That means you have to return to silence, constantly, of *gassho*; you have to return to silence of having a meal, constantly. That is called to *employ the true human with no position*. Because at the very incipient moment, there is no position, no name, no perceptions.

So, that is, here it says, 

> ... it is not “I,” it is not *who*; ...

There is nothing to call *I*, also there is nothing to call *who*, because you are there. You are there, at that time you are simultaneously the very incipient moment. So the very incipient moment doesn’t appear *in* your life; you are exactly *there*, alone. 

> ... therefore being *not compulsory* is called *on its own*. 

So, nothing to force you to do by the self or by others; nothing there. But you are constantly qualified to *be*, and work. So that’s being *not compulsory* is called *on its own*. This is the self.

This is called *jinen* (自然) in Japanese. *Jinen* means same thing as *shizen*: nature; *shizen* is called sometimes in the Buddhistic way *jinen*. *Ji* of *jinen* is the self, or of itself; that is *ji* of *jinen*. According to this translation ji is “own.” In Japanese, *ji* has two meanings, one is “the self” and also “of itself.” 

So, *nen* is “ought to be.” *Ought to be* means nothing to force something to be. Naturally, as it is; or what is just is; this is *nen* of *jinen*. That is [also] called *shizen*, nature. Generally we call it *shizen*, which means nature, or natural. 

#### 56:46

So, next is:

> *Accord* is permission. 

So *nen* translated as “accord,” which means what is just is. So *accord* means the permission. Permission means a state of being which you fit into the great energy of being, arising constantly at the very incipient moment. That is called *dharma*, we say; *dharma* or *Buddha*, we say. 

So, if you fit into it, this is called *accord*, or *what is just is*. So, your existence becomes *what is just is*. Natural. *As-it-is-ness*, or we call it *thusness*.

So, that’s why he says, “*Accord* is permission.” So, 

> *Ripening of its own accord* is the time of the flower’s opening and forming fruit, ...

So, ripening of its own accord means what? Ripening of its own accord means when you do *gassho*, this is a ripening of its own accord, so-called *gassho*, which is nothing but the great energy of being before you put the name on it. So when it appears under certain circumstances it is called *gassho*. 

So this *gassho* is called *effect*, because *effect* originates from *cause* [which is] called *Buddha*, great energy of being. And then, if you fit into this rhythm of life, then you are being who is permissible to receive. That is permission. When you become as you really are, you are permitted to be – day to day, beyond human speculation. And then it makes your life mature.

So, very naturally, next it says “forming of fruit.” So, “*Ripening of its own accord* is the time of the flower’s opening and forming fruit.” “Flower’s opening” means, flowers of *gassho*, flowers of *bodhi-mind*, flowers of having a meal... many flowers opening, day to day. And then it must be *ripened*, as a result. That is forming a fruit. 

And then you have to really fit into the time of the very incipient moment. This is a great time where the flower of the *gassho* opens and forms its own result. Abd *then* you become mature; you grow, naturally. That is a time, opportunity; that is aware, right now. 

So, practice is *right now*, day to day. If you do it, your practice *right now*, extending into every inch of the universe. Then, the self becomes the whole universe, we can say. 

And then at that time, 

> ... it is the time of *communicating the Teaching to save deluded sentient beings*.

*Then*, when you handle this time, great opportunity – at that time, through this time and opportunity, you can *teach* how sublime human beings are. And also, simultaneously, people feel relief. If they see this kind of person, naturally [they feel] relief, before they talk about Buddha’s teaching. That is called “to save deluded sentient beings.” 

So very naturally, that person, wherever they may go, the place where he stands becomes peaceful. People around him become peaceful. 

If you read the *agamas* about the Buddha’s life, very often you can see this kind of Buddha’s life. Wherever he may go, people felt completely peaceful, and naturally, they made their head low, and bowed to him, before he began to talk. That happened when he met his friend, the five bhikkhus, five monks, after he came down from the mountain. Do you remember? This is the beginning of the Sangha. 

This is a Buddha. So, very often you can read, you can see this statement in the *agamas*, or other Buddhist scriptures. 

So, if you want to practice Buddha’s teaching, I think we must be this kind of person, called Buddha. So day to day, we become Buddha.
